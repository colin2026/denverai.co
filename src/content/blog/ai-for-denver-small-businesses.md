Every week there's a new AI tool promising to transform your business. Most of them are built for large companies with dedicated IT teams and six-figure software budgets. If you run a small or mid-sized business in Denver, here's what's actually worth paying attention to - and what you can skip.

## The problem with most AI advice

Most articles about AI for small business are written by people who've never run one. They describe features, not outcomes. They show demos, not day-to-day realities.

The question that matters isn't "can AI do this?" - it's "will my team actually use it, and will it save us real time?"

---

## What consistently works for Denver SMBs

### Automating follow-up

The single highest-impact use of AI for most small businesses is automating the follow-up that falls through the cracks. New lead comes in at 9pm on a Friday? AI sends an immediate response and books the call. Job finishes and no one sent the follow-up? AI handles it.

This works especially well for contractors, real estate agents, medical and dental practices, and any business where speed-to-response matters.

### Admin work nobody wants to do

Invoices, intake forms, scheduling, reminders - these are the things that eat 10-15 hours a week across a typical team. AI doesn't replace the work, it handles it automatically so your people focus on the job.

### Customer-facing responsiveness

After-hours inquiries, status update requests, basic questions - AI can handle a surprising amount of this without it feeling robotic, if set up correctly.

---

## What's usually not worth it yet

Complex decision-making, creative strategy, anything that requires real judgment about your specific customers or context - these still need humans. Using AI to write all your marketing copy without editing it tends to produce content that sounds generic and off-brand.

The sweet spot is repetitive, rule-based work with clear inputs and outputs. That's where AI earns its keep.

---

## What it actually takes to implement

The tools themselves aren't the hard part. The hard part is:

- Mapping which processes are actually worth automating
- Connecting the AI to the software you already use
- Getting your team to trust and use it consistently

That's what most AI consultants don't talk about. Implementation without adoption is just expensive software that nobody uses.
